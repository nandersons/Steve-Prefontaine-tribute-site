Steve "Pre" Prefontaine

"To give anything less than your best is to sacrifice the gift."

-Steve Prefontaine

Steve Prefontaine runs at the University of Oregon's historic "Hayward Field" located in Eugene, Oregon.

Here's a timeline of Steve Prefontaine's life:

  • 1951 - Born in Coos Bay, Oregon
  • 1965 - Starts school Marshfield High School and joins the cross country team where he is coached by Walt

    • McClure, Jr.

  • 1968 - Goes entire junior year of cross country undefeated
  • 1969 - Runs the US high school national record in the 2-mile in 8:41 and again goes undefeated
  • 1969 - Signs letter of intent to attend the University of Oregon under legendary coach Bill Bowerman
  • 1970 - In his four years at Oregon, never loses an NCAA race at 3 miles, 5,000 meters, 6 miles, or 10,000

    • meters. Also, wins three NCAA DI Cross Country titles and four straight 3 mile/5,000 meter titles in track

  • 1970 - Appears on the cover of Sports Illustrated at the age of 19
  • 1972 - Sets American records in the 5,000 and 3,000 meter runs and competes at the Olympics getting 4th
  • 1973 - Sets 6 more American records and ends his collegiate career as an Oregon Duck
  • 1974 - Begins a lengthy fight with the Amature Athletic Union (AAU) which demanded that athletes who wished to

    • retain status as "amature" for the Olympics not be paid for appearances in track meets

  • 1975 - After attending a post-race party, is killed in a car accident while driving home after swerving into a rock

    • wall in getting pinned beneath his own vehicle

  • 1975 - The University of Oregon creates a track meet, The Prefontaine Classic, to honor Pre's memory
  • 1983 - inducted into the Oregon Sports Hall of Fame
  • 1997/1998 - Steve Prefontaines life story is detailed in the feature films Prefontaine starring Jared Leto as

    • Prefontaine, and Without Limits starring Billy Crudup as Prefontaine

  • 2005 - Nike places a memorial ad in Sports Illustrated and on TV for the 30th anniversary of his death

"Pre didn’t have the classic stature of a distance runner. He was small. One leg was

shorter than the other. But he lived to run, and that passion made him one of the
sport’s great champions."

-Nike CEO, Mark Parker
